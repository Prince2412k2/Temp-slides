## Slide 2: What Is Project Management?

**Definition:** Project management is the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to meet project requirements and achieve the desired outcome.

Key activities every project manager performs:
- Planning and organizing
- Managing tasks
- Budgeting and controlling costs
- Ensuring delivery on time and within scope

**Why it matters:** Businesses depend on project management to deliver expected outcomes on time and within budget. Without it, projects risk missing goals, overspending, or failing entirely.

---

## Slide 3: What Does a Project Manager Actually Do?

Project managers follow a structured process but wear many hats:

- Use productivity tools and create processes
- Create project plans, schedules, and budgets
- Manage and communicate with stakeholders
- Identify and manage risks
- Guide the team from initiation through closing
- Remove obstacles blocking team progress

**Key mindset:** A project manager does not do all the work directly. They support the people who do.

## Slide 4: The Value of a Project Manager

Project managers add value through:

**Prioritization** - deciding what the team should focus on first

**Delegation** - assigning work to the right people at the right time

**Effective communication** - serving as the main information hub for the team and stakeholders

**Organizational and interpersonal skills** - keeping everyone aligned and moving forward

Project managers shepherd projects from start to finish, serving as guides using impeccable organizational and interpersonal skills every step of the way.

## Slide 6: Project Structures and Life Cycles

**The Project Life Cycle** consists of four phases:

1. **Initiation** - define the project, identify stakeholders, create the project charter
2. **Planning** - develop the project plan, set schedule, identify tasks and milestones
3. **Execution** - carry out the plan, manage the team, track progress
4. **Closing** - formally complete the project, document lessons learned, hand off deliverables

Understanding the life cycle provides a structure that guides the project manager no matter how different each project is.

## Slide 7: Organizational Structure and Company Culture

**Types of organizational structures:**
- **Classic/Functional** - team members report to functional managers, PM has less authority
- **Matrix** - team members report to both a functional manager and a project manager
- **Project-based** - team members are dedicated to projects, PM has more authority

**Why culture matters:**
Organizational culture is based on shared workplace values. It shows up in how people communicate, make decisions, and work together.

A project that is out of sync with the company's culture is much harder to complete successfully.

Culture influences:

- How teams communicate
- How conflict is handled
- How change is accepted

**Practical impact for PMs:**
- Understand who has authority and who needs to be consulted
- Adapt communication style to fit the culture
- Use the culture's strengths to move the project forward
- Recognize when cultural barriers may slow progress and address them proactively

## Slide 9: Key Parts of Project Initiation

Initiation is where you set the foundation. Key components:

- **Goals** - what you are trying to accomplish
- **Scope** - what is and is not included in the project
- **Deliverables** - the tangible outcomes you will produce
- **Success criteria** - how you will know the project succeeded
- **Stakeholders** - everyone who has an interest in the outcome
- **Resources** - people, budget, and materials needed

Poor preparation during initiation creates problems throughout the entire project.

---

## Slide 10: Setting SMART Goals

Goals must be **SMART** to be actionable:

- **Specific** - clearly defined, not vague
- **Measurable** - has metrics or indicators of success
- **Attainable** - realistic and achievable
- **Relevant** - aligned with the broader business objective
- **Time-bound** - has a defined deadline or timeframe

**Example:** "Increase customer satisfaction scores by 15% within 6 months by launching a new follow-up survey system" is SMART.

"Improve customer experience" is not SMART.

---

## Slide 11: Scope and Scope Creep

**Project scope** defines what is included and what is not included in the project.

**Scope creep** occurs when tasks or features are added to the project without a corresponding change to the timeline, budget, or resources.

How to prevent scope creep:
- Clearly document what is in scope at the start
- Get sign-off from stakeholders on the scope
- Have a formal change request process
- Push back on additions that are not approved

Scope creep is one of the most common reasons projects go over budget and miss deadlines.

---

## Slide 12: Communicating and Working with Stakeholders

**Stakeholders** are anyone who has an interest in the project outcome, including sponsors, customers, team members, and other departments.

Steps to manage stakeholders effectively:
1. Identify all stakeholders early
2. Understand their interests and influence levels
3. Create a stakeholder map (RACI chart)
4. Communicate regularly based on their needs
5. Involve them at key decision points

**RACI Chart:** Defines who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed for each task.

## Slide 13: The Benefits of Project Planning

The planning phase is critical because it helps you:

- Document scope, tasks, milestones, and budget
- Set a realistic schedule
- Identify risks before they happen
- Align the team and stakeholders on expectations
- Create a roadmap for execution

**What goes into a project plan:**
- Tasks and milestones
- Assigned team members
- Documentation and resources
- Timeline and deadlines

Lack of planning is one of the top causes of project failure.

---

## Slide 14: Project Planning for Beginners

**The five core elements of a project plan:**

1. **Tasks** - activities that need to be completed within a set time
2. **Milestones** - important points that indicate progress or phase completion
3. **People** - who is responsible for what
4. **Documentation** - supporting materials, charters, contracts, requirements
5. **Time** - estimates for each task and an overall schedule

**At the center of every project plan is the schedule.** The schedule is your guide for time estimates, milestone tracking, and overall project progress.

---

## Slide 15: Building a Project Plan (Google Sheets)

Project plans can be built in a spreadsheet with the following approach:

1. Review the project charter goals and deliverables
2. List all tasks associated with each deliverable
3. Identify milestones within that task list
4. Estimate time for each task
5. Assign owners
6. Track progress continuously

**Starting point:** Ask yourself for each deliverable, "What steps do we need to take to achieve this?" Those steps become your tasks.

Analyze emails, old project plans, and stakeholder conversations to uncover additional tasks you may have missed.

---

## Slide 16: Negotiating with Stakeholders and Planning

Strong project plans require buy-in. Key negotiation principles:

- Understand what stakeholders truly need, not just what they ask for
- Be transparent about constraints (time, budget, scope)
- Offer alternatives when you cannot deliver exactly what is requested
- Document all agreements to avoid misunderstandings later
- Revisit the plan if circumstances change

**Empathy is a negotiation tool.** When you understand the stakeholder's underlying goal, you can find creative solutions that work for everyone.

## Slide 17: How to Create a Project Budget

**A project budget** is the estimated monetary resources needed to achieve the project's goals and objectives.

Budget components:
- Labor costs
- Materials and supplies
- Software and tools
- Vendor and contractor fees
- Contingency reserves for unexpected costs

**Budgeting steps:**
1. Break down costs by milestone and deliverable
2. Use historical data from similar projects
3. Get estimates from vendors and team members
4. Build in a buffer for risk
5. Track spending continuously throughout the project

## Slide 18: Risk Management Basics

**Risk** is any potential event that could impact the project positively or negatively.

**Risk management process:**
1. Identify risks - brainstorm what could go wrong
2. Assess likelihood and impact of each risk
3. Prioritize risks that are both likely and high impact
4. Create a mitigation plan for top risks
5. Monitor risks throughout the project

**Types of risks:**
- Schedule risks (delays)
- Budget risks (cost overruns)
- Scope risks (uncontrolled changes)
- Resource risks (people leaving, unavailability)
- External risks (market shifts, regulation)

No project goes 100% according to plan. Flexibility and preparation are essential.

Figuring out resources early prevents understaffing and avoids delays. The right tools make it easy for the whole team to stay on track and on the same page.

## Slide 20: The Project Communication Plan

**A communication plan** is a document that outlines:
- Who needs to receive information
- What type of information they need
- How often they need it
- Through which channel (email, meeting, report)
- Who is responsible for sending it

Why communication plans matter:
- Prevents missed updates
- Ensures everyone knows what is happening
- Reduces confusion and rework
- Helps manage stakeholder expectations

Communication is arguably the most important tool in project management. Most project failures involve a communication breakdown.

---

## Slide 21: Communicate Effectively as a Project Manager

Project managers spend approximately 90% of their time on communication.

**Effective communication is:**
- Clear
- Honest
- Relevant
- Frequent but not excessive (avoid information overload)

**Communication channels to master:**
- Email (keep it concise, clear subject lines, appropriate recipients)
- Meetings (structured, intentional, agenda-driven)
- Instant messaging (quick updates, not for complex discussions)
- Written documents and reports

Communicate the same important information multiple times and in multiple ways, since people absorb information differently.

## Slide 22: How to Run a Successful Meeting

**The four elements of an effective meeting:**

1. **Structured** - starts and ends on time, topics are prioritized, a notetaker is assigned
2. **Intentional** - has a clear purpose and a prepared agenda
3. **Collaborative** - participants are encouraged to contribute
4. **Inclusive** - the right people are invited, and everyone has a chance to speak

**Tips:**
- Send the agenda in advance
- Only invite people who need to be there
- Assign action items with owners and deadlines
- Send a follow-up summary after the meeting

---

## Slide 23: How to Organize Effective Meetings

**Pre-meeting preparation:**
- Define the purpose of the meeting
- Build an agenda
- Share the agenda with attendees beforehand
- Book the right time and location (or video link)

**During the meeting:**
- Stick to the agenda
- Manage time actively
- Record decisions and action items

**After the meeting:**
- Send a summary with action items, owners, and due dates
- Follow up on unresolved items

Not every issue requires a meeting. Consider whether an email or async update could accomplish the same goal.

## Slide 24: Project Tracking Methods and Examples

During execution, project managers track progress to ensure the project stays on course.

**Common tracking methods:**

- **Gantt chart** - visual timeline showing tasks, durations, and dependencies
- **Kanban board** - visual board with columns for To Do, In Progress, and Done
- **Burndown chart** - tracks how much work remains versus time available
- **Status reports** - regular written updates shared with stakeholders

The tracking method you choose should match the complexity of the project and the needs of your team and stakeholders.

## Slide 25: Effective Project Management During Execution

During execution, a project manager's focus shifts to:

- Keeping the team on track and unblocked
- Managing risks as they arise
- Maintaining open communication with stakeholders
- Monitoring scope, schedule, and budget
- Updating the project plan as things change

**Key principle:** The project plan is a living document. It should be updated whenever there is a significant change to scope, schedule, or budget, with all changes clearly communicated to the team.

## Slide 26: Leadership vs Management in a Successful Team

**Management** focuses on the logistics - tasks, schedules, budgets, processes.

**Leadership** focuses on people - inspiring, motivating, supporting, and empowering the team.

The best project managers do both. They handle the logistics while also creating an environment where people can do their best work.

**A team** is a group of people who plan, solve problems, make decisions, and review their progress together to achieve a common goal.

The project manager's ability to build and lead a team that works well together is one of the most important factors in project success.

## Slide 28: How Project Managers Can Influence Those Around Them

**Influencing** is the ability to alter another person's thinking or behaviors without direct authority.

**The four steps of effective influence:**

1. **Establish credibility** - show expertise and trustworthiness
2. **Frame for common ground** - show how your idea benefits them
3. **Provide evidence** - use data and examples to support your case
4. **Connect emotionally** - make the case resonate on a personal level

Influence is a critical skill for project managers who often need to motivate people who do not report to them directly.

Customer satisfaction is a key measure of project success.

- Communicate proactively and transparently

**Soft skills to practice:**

## Slide 30: Quality Management and Continuous Improvement

**Quality** means fulfilling the outlined requirements for the deliverable and meeting or exceeding the needs and expectations of your customer.

The **triple constraint** of project management:
- **Scope** - what the project includes
- **Time** - when it will be delivered
- **Budget** - what it will cost

If any one element suffers, overall quality suffers too. The PM must balance all three.

**Continuous improvement** is an ongoing effort to improve products, processes, or services. It involves recognizing what needs to change, planning for it, and implementing improvements.

---

## Slide 31: Determining Your Project's Success

**Quality management planning** happens before execution begins. It ensures the team knows what standards to meet.

Key activities:
- Define quality standards for each deliverable
- Identify how quality will be evaluated
- Conduct quality reviews and inspections during the project
- Document and address any quality gaps
- Present quality findings to stakeholders regularly

**Retrospective documents** are used during project execution to review progress and identify process improvements before the project ends.

## Slide 32: Storytelling with Data to Make Decisions

Data-driven decision making is a key modern PM skill.

**Using data in projects:**
- Identify what questions you need to answer
- Determine which data will answer those questions
- Gather and analyze the data
- Present findings in a clear, compelling way

**Data storytelling principles:**
- Lead with the insight, not the raw numbers
- Use visuals (charts, graphs) to make data accessible
- Connect data back to the project's goals
- Be transparent about limitations and uncertainties

Good data storytelling helps stakeholders understand what is happening and make informed decisions.

## Slide 33: How and When to Close a Project

**Project closing** is the formal process of completing the project, the current phase, and all contractual obligations.

**Three criteria for a project to be officially closed:**
1. All work has been completed and approved
2. Stakeholders have accepted the deliverables
3. Administrative tasks (documentation, handoffs) are finished

Finishing tasks is not the same as closing the project. There are always wrap-up steps to complete.

---

## Slide 34: How to Write the Close Out Report

**The close-out report** documents the entire project lifecycle and captures:

- Project summary and objectives
- Summary of what was delivered versus what was planned
- Key milestones and their completion status
- Budget summary (planned vs. actual)
- Risk log and how risks were handled
- Lessons learned from the project
- Final project outcomes and success criteria evaluation

The close-out report benefits future projects by preserving institutional knowledge and helping teams avoid repeating mistakes.

## Slide 35: What Is Agile Project Management?

**Agile** is not a single methodology. It is an overarching approach and philosophy to delivering value to customers.

**Agile vs. Waterfall:**

| Waterfall | Agile |
|---|---|
| Linear and sequential | Iterative and flexible |
| Requirements fixed upfront | Requirements evolve with feedback |
| Heavy documentation | Just enough documentation |
| Deliverable at the end | Frequent small deliverables |
| Resistant to change | Embraces change |

Agile emerged organically in the software industry in the 1990s and was formalized in 2001 with the Agile Manifesto.

---

## Slide 36: The Agile Manifesto

Written in 2001 by thought leaders in software development, the Agile Manifesto establishes four core values:

1. **Individuals and interactions** over processes and tools
2. **Working software** over comprehensive documentation
3. **Customer collaboration** over contract negotiation
4. **Responding to change** over following a plan

The right side still has value. The left side is valued more.

These four values define the mindset every Agile team should strive for.

---

## Slide 37: The 12 Agile Principles (Grouped by Theme)

The 12 principles fall into four themes:

**1. Value Delivery** - Deliver working solutions frequently, simplify, focus on what matters most

**2. Business Collaboration** - Keep developers and business people working closely together; welcome changing requirements

**3. Team Dynamics and Culture** - Build motivated teams, trust them, give them what they need, support sustainable pace

**4. Retrospectives and Continuous Learning** - At regular intervals, reflect on how to improve and adjust accordingly

## Slide 38: What Is VUCA and How Does It Help Project Managers?

**VUCA** is a framework developed by the US Military War College to describe forces affecting organizations in a changing world.

- **Volatility** - the rate of change; disruptions come frequently
- **Uncertainty** - lack of predictability; hard to plan with confidence
- **Complexity** - many interrelated forces affecting the project
- **Ambiguity** - difficulty identifying root causes of events

If your project environment has high levels of VUCA, Agile is likely the better approach.

Agile does not eliminate VUCA but gives teams tools and systems to navigate it effectively.

## Slide 39: The Advantages of Agile

**Why teams choose Agile:**

- Faster feedback from customers reduces the risk of building the wrong thing
- Frequent small deliveries create continuous value
- Teams are more adaptable to market and customer changes
- Collaboration replaces excessive documentation
- Sustainable work pace reduces burnout
- Continuous improvement through retrospectives

Agile works best in environments with high uncertainty, changing requirements, and a need to deliver value quickly and frequently.

## Slide 40: Introduction to Agile Methodologies

Several specific frameworks fall under the Agile umbrella:

**Scrum** - the most popular, uses sprints, backlog, and defined roles

**Kanban** - visual board showing workflow; limits work in progress to maximize flow

**Extreme Programming (XP)** - takes software development best practices to an extreme, including pair programming and test-first development

**Lean** - originated in Toyota manufacturing; eliminates waste and focuses on delivering value

Each framework applies Agile values and principles in a different way. Teams can blend them.

---

## Slide 41: What Are the 5 Principles of Lean Methodology?

Lean methodology, which pre-dates Agile and directly inspired it, is built on five principles:

1. **Define Value** - identify what the customer wants and include them in the process
2. **Map Value Stream** - map every step in producing value; eliminate wasteful steps
3. **Create Flow** - ensure the product moves through the value stream efficiently; remove delays
4. **Establish Pull** - make the process so smooth customers can request deliverables at any time
5. **Pursue Perfection** - continuously improve all four steps above

Agile borrowed heavily from Lean, especially the focus on eliminating waste and delivering customer value.

---

## Slide 42: What Is Scrum?

Scrum is a framework for developing, delivering, and sustaining complex products even in unpredictable environments.

**The three pillars of Scrum:**
1. **Transparency** - everyone involved can see the process and progress
2. **Inspection** - frequent review of the work and the process
3. **Adaptation** - adjusting the process based on what inspections reveal

**The five Scrum values:**
- Commitment
- Courage
- Focus
- Openness
- Respect

Scrum is not an acronym. It comes from a rugby formation where players lock together and push as a unit.

---

## Slide 43: Scrum Roles and Responsibilities

**Product Owner**
- Owns the product backlog
- Prioritizes work based on customer and business value
- Has final say on what the team builds
- Represents the product to stakeholders

**Scrum Master**
- Ensures the team follows Agile values and Scrum practices
- Removes obstacles blocking the team
- Coaches the team on continuous improvement
- Is not a traditional manager

**Development Team**
- Cross-functional group (3 to 9 people)
- Self-organizing: decides how to do the work
- Collectively responsible for the sprint deliverables

---

## Slide 44: Scrum Events (Ceremonies)

**Sprint** - a time-boxed period of 1 to 4 weeks during which the team completes a set of work; most sprints are 2 weeks

**Sprint Planning** - the team selects items from the backlog and commits to completing them during the sprint

**Daily Scrum (Standup)** - a 15-minute daily meeting where the team reports progress, plans for the day, and flags blockers

**Sprint Review** - the team demos completed work to stakeholders at the end of the sprint

**Sprint Retrospective** - the team reflects on how they worked and identifies improvements for the next sprint

---

## Slide 45: The Product Backlog

**The product backlog** is the single authoritative source for all work the team will do. It contains:

- Features
- Requirements
- Bug fixes
- Improvements
- Any other tasks needed to deliver the product

**Key characteristics:**
- It is a living artifact - items are added, removed, or reprioritized at any time
- The product owner is responsible for maintaining and prioritizing it
- Items at the top of the backlog are the most refined and ready to work on
- Items at the bottom are less defined and lower priority

---

## Slide 46: Scrum Master Tutorial (Managing the Backlog)

**Backlog refinement** is the ongoing process of reviewing and improving backlog items:
- Add detail, estimates, and acceptance criteria
- Split large items into smaller, more manageable ones
- Remove items that are no longer relevant
- Re-prioritize based on new information

**User stories** are a common format for backlog items:
"As a [user type], I want [action] so that [benefit]."

User stories keep the focus on customer value rather than technical implementation.

## Slide 47: What Projects Benefit from an Agile Approach?

Agile is well suited for projects where:
- Requirements are unclear or expected to change
- Customer feedback is needed early and often
- The market or technology environment is fast-moving
- The team is small, cross-functional, and collaborative
- Innovation and experimentation are encouraged

Agile is less suited for projects where:
- Requirements are fixed and well understood
- Regulatory or contractual constraints require heavy documentation
- Deliverables are highly sequential with few feedback loops

No approach is universally superior. The best PMs know when to use which approach.

---

## Slide 48: When Is Agile Best to Use?

Ask these questions to decide if Agile is right for your project:

- Is the environment volatile, uncertain, complex, or ambiguous (VUCA)?
- Will requirements likely change as the project progresses?
- Can the team work in short iterations and deliver something of value frequently?
- Is the customer available to give regular feedback?
- Is the team open to self-organizing and continuous improvement?

If the answers are mostly yes, Agile or a hybrid Agile approach will likely serve the project well.

---

## Slide 49: What Are Blending Methods in Project Management?

Many real-world projects use a hybrid of Agile and traditional (Waterfall) approaches.

**Reasons to blend methods:**
- Stakeholders prefer traditional reporting but the team works in sprints
- Some phases require sequential execution; others benefit from iteration
- Regulatory requirements demand formal documentation
- Vendors on the project follow a Waterfall approach

**Examples of blending:**
- A Scrum team uses a Kanban board to track sprint work
- A team uses XP pair programming within a Scrum sprint
- A Waterfall project adopts Agile retrospectives to improve team performance

---

## Slide 50: Agile and Scrum in the Real World - Value Driven Delivery

**Value-driven delivery** means the team is always focused on delivering the highest possible value to the user.

Three questions to guide this:

1. **Are we building the right thing?** - Do we truly understand what the customer wants and needs?

2. **Are we building it right?** - Are we only building approved features and avoiding unnecessary complexity?

3. **Are we running it right?** - Have we thought through how users will interact with the product after delivery and how to support them?

All three must work together to ensure continuous delivery of value throughout the product lifecycle.

---

## Slide 51: The Value Roadmap

**A value roadmap** maps out the timelines and requirements for product development and guides the team toward maximum value delivery.

**Three components of a value roadmap:**

1. **Product Vision** - defines what the product is, who it serves, and how it supports the business strategy

2. **Product Roadmap** - a high-level view of expected features, requirements, and estimated timeline for milestones

3. **Release Plans** - approximate dates when specific features will be delivered to users; only the first release date should be considered firm

The roadmap is a living document and should be reviewed at least quarterly.

---

## Slide 52: Agile Management - Challenges and Solutions

**Common Agile challenges and how to address them:**

**Value delivery issues** - team misses deadlines, tasks pile up, burnout appears
- Solution: demo work more often, focus on fewer user stories per sprint, clarify what "done" means

**Business collaboration issues** - teams receive overwhelming feedback, "us vs. them" mentality develops
- Solution: more frequent demos, solution design sprints, manage backlog changes between sprints only

**Team dynamics issues** - low morale, high conflict, or suspiciously no conflict
- Solution: team brainstorm on working better together, change workflows, try retrospective techniques like Six Hats Thinking

---

## Slide 53: Agile and Scrum Pitfalls

**Managing a stable product roadmap:**
- Product ambition: product owner overpromises to stakeholders
- Solution: agree upfront on how new opportunities are reviewed; hold quarterly roadmap reviews; share knowledge between PO and development team

**Incomplete Scrum implementation:**
- Skipping events or blending roles reduces transparency and benefits
- Solution: implement Scrum completely; ensure roles are clearly defined and filled by the right people

**Lack of team stability:**
- Frequent team turnover disrupts flow and velocity
- Solution: quick onboarding, pair programming for new members, shorter sprints during periods of change

---

## Slide 54: The Scrum Master as Agile Coach

The Scrum master functions like a sports coach for the team:

**1. Design the plays** - work with the team to build a shared playbook for how sprints, reviews, and daily work operate

**2. Provide feedback** - give guidance in the moment and from a big-picture view, not just during retrospectives

**3. Celebrate and learn** - acknowledge wins frequently; treat setbacks as learning data rather than failures

As Thomas Edison said, "I have not failed. I've just found 10,000 ways that won't work."

The Scrum master's goal is to continuously help the team improve and become the best version of itself.

## Slide 55: Agile's Evolution and Future

Since 2001, Agile has evolved significantly:

**DevOps** - combines software development and IT operations; focuses on delivering software continuously with high quality and reliability

**Business Agility** - applying Agile principles beyond IT to finance, HR, governance, and entire organizations; frameworks like SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) and Scrum of Scrums support large-scale adoption

**Agile beyond software:**
- Healthcare, construction, education, finance, and sales teams now use Agile approaches
- Even personal projects (moving, party planning) can benefit from Kanban boards

85% of organizations have adopted a product-centric model associated with Agile approaches.

- Build a network of peers to learn from
- Take on stretch assignments to develop new skills

**Process improvement** means identifying, analyzing, and improving existing workflows to enhance team performance and optimize outcomes for customers.

A great project manager never stops learning.
